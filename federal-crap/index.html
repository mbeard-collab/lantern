Executive Summary

Federal cyber is the largest and most contested capability buy in government technology.

The federal cyber and IT modernization market is on a multi-year buying wave that does not show signs of slowing. Zero trust implementation, JADC2 connectivity, network modernization, software factories, identity and access management, and AI-enabled defensive operations are all funded as separate priorities, and they all converge on the same buyer base. The result is a procurement environment with more dollars in motion, more vendors competing, and more capability complexity than at any point in the last two decades.

The DoD owns most of the spend, but the buyer map is wider than that. DISA and JFHQ-DODIN run the network. USCYBERCOM and the service cyber components run the operations. NSA shapes the technical baseline. CISA, GSA, and the federal civilian agencies are running their own zero trust and modernization arcs. Each of those buyers is competing for the same cleared workforce and the same set of tier-one integrators.

Vendors that win consistently in 2026 understand the program structure. They know where DoD CIO direction translates into DISA acquisition. They know which Other Transactions and Software Acquisition Pathway efforts are real. They know which sustainment lines are about to roll over and which integrator is incumbent. And they know how to deliver capability at the cadence the operators expect, not the cadence the FAR allows.

This report walks through the market shape, the priorities reshaping every program, the buyer map across DoD and federal civilian, and a closer look at where federal cyber dollars are actually moving. Federal data throughout was pulled live from the GovSpend MCP.

Section 2 · What is reshaping federal cyber

Seven priorities will define how cyber tech companies compete in 2026.

These are the shifts already showing up in DoD CIO direction, DISA solicitations, service cyber budget submissions, and federal civilian zero trust roadmaps.

Force 01

Zero trust moves from strategy to delivery

The DoD Zero Trust Strategy and target activities have been live for multiple budget cycles. Programs like Thunderdome, ICAM modernization, and microsegmentation pilots are now in production at scale. Federal civilian agencies are tracking against OMB M-22-09 deadlines and Phase 2 implementations.

Vendors that can show real fielded zero trust outcomes, not slideware, are taking share. The shortlist is moving toward identity-first, data-centric architectures backed by automated policy and continuous validation.

Force 02

JADC2 and CJADC2 reshape the network buy

Joint and combined all-domain command and control are no longer concept. Service-level efforts like the Air Force's ABMS, Army's PC-C2, Navy's Project Overmatch, and joint-level integration are funded and competing for the same network and data fabric talent.

This is creating sustained pull for resilient transport, sensor-to-shooter data fabric, mission partner connectivity, and tactical edge compute. Cyber and network vendors that can position into JADC2 architecture, not just adjacent capabilities, are landing on multi-year programs.

Force 03

AI takes its first real seat in the SOC

2026 is the year AI moves from analyst augmentation to operational decision support inside cyber operations centers. Detection, triage, threat hunting, and response orchestration are all seeing operationalized AI deployments at the service cyber commands and inside CISA.

The buyers reward speed of integration with existing SIEM, SOAR, and XDR investments, not greenfield AI tooling. Software vendors with strong APIs, on-prem and hybrid deployment options, and cleared engineering benches are pulling ahead.

Force 04

Software factories become procurement infrastructure

Platform One, Black Pearl, Kessel Run, BESPIN, Kobayashi Maru, and the broader DoD software factory ecosystem have stopped being projects. They are now procurement infrastructure that downstream programs build against. ATO acceleration, container baselines, and DevSecOps pipelines are explicit RFP requirements.

For software vendors, that means winning means being deployable inside an existing factory, not asking the buyer to stand up a new one. For services firms, it means building software factory enablement as a core practice.

Force 05

Cloud at the edge and JWCC reshape the platform layer

The Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability is now the default vehicle for DoD cloud purchases, and it is being matched by service-level edge cloud efforts that push compute closer to operations. Federal civilian agencies continue migrating workloads under FedRAMP High and StateRAMP for state interoperability.

The competitive bar has moved from cloud capacity to cloud workload portability, security automation, and certified data protection. Vendors that can document continuous compliance and run across multiple clouds win infrastructure decisions faster.

Force 06

Identity is the contested capability of the next budget cycle

ICAM modernization, non-person entity identity, machine-to-machine credentialing, and cross-domain identity federation are getting the funding profile that endpoint security got five years ago. Identity is now treated as the foundational control surface for zero trust, JADC2, and cloud.

This is creating room for identity-native vendors to displace bolt-on solutions. Federal buyers are paying close attention to FedRAMP-authorized identity stacks, ICAM-as-a-service offerings, and PIV-derived credential modernization.

Force 07

Cyber and electromagnetic warfare keep converging

EW, cyber, and signals operations are increasingly planned and resourced together. Service-level investments in convergence, multi-domain operations, and software-defined radios are pulling traditional cyber vendors into spectrum and EW conversations, and pulling EW vendors into cyber conversations.

Vendors that can speak fluently across both domains, with hardware that hosts software, software that fuses RF and packet data, and operators that train across both, are the ones winning emerging convergence programs.

Section 5 · What separates winners

Five things the fastest growing federal cyber companies are doing right now.

These show up in obligation patterns and award patterns regardless of company size. They are the operating habits of the firms pulling away in the AFCEA Cyber community.

  • 1

    They deliver against the existing software factory ecosystem

    Vendors that show up to a program with a working pipeline against Platform One, Black Pearl, Kessel Run, or BESPIN are clearing technical reviews their competitors are still trying to schedule. Winning means deployable in weeks, not months.

  • 2

    They lead identity and zero trust with measurable outcomes

    Buyers are tired of zero trust slideware. The companies winning programs are leading with concrete metrics: time to revoke, identity verification latency, segmented blast radius, percent of workloads under continuous validation. That kind of substantiation moves shortlists.

  • 3

    They build a cleared workforce strategy on purpose

    The federal cyber workforce constraint is real. Vendors that can demonstrate a stable bench of cleared engineers, trusted subcontractors, and a recruiting machine that keeps up with attrition are favored over ones with better tech but thinner staffing.

  • 4

    They use the right vehicle, not a favorite vehicle

    JWCC, ASTRO, OASIS+, GSA MAS, CIO-SP4, Polaris, ENCORE III, and program-specific IDIQs each have a fit. Winners match the vehicle to the buyer, not the other way around. This shows up clearly in award patterns over the last 24 months.

  • 5

    They time outreach to real signals

    Sustainment renewals, expiring task orders, recompete announcements, and DoD CIO direction memos all show when a buyer is in market. Vendors that build BD around those signals reach the right contracting officer and program manager earlier than the rest of the field.

Data & Methodology Notes

Federal data: All federal cyber and IT modernization data was pulled live from the GovSpend MCP on May 4, 2026, filtered to a keyword set covering cybersecurity, cyber operations, zero trust, USCYBERCOM, ARCYBER, AFCYBER, DISA, JFHQ-DODIN, JADC2, DevSecOps, ICAM, network modernization, mission partner environment, and incident response. The keyword set is intentionally broad to capture cyber and IT modernization activity across DoD and federal civilian, but narrow enough to exclude unrelated federal IT work. Figures are directional and represent a meaningful slice of the federal cyber enterprise, not a total addressable market.

Obligated amounts: $26B+ in tracked obligations reflects the sum of `dollarValues.obligatedAmount.value` on FPDS records matching the keyword set across May 4, 2024 through May 4, 2026. Negative obligations from contract closeouts and de-obligations are included as recorded in FPDS.

Open opportunities: 461 open federal cyber opportunities reflects active SAM.gov solicitations as of May 4, 2026 matching the same keyword set with response deadlines still open. Classified or limited-distribution opportunities are not represented in public SAM.gov.

Place of performance: State-level figures reflect principal place of performance recorded in FPDS. Performance can occur away from the contracting office.

Vendor figures: Some vendors appear under multiple legal entity names in FPDS. Booz Allen Hamilton, for example, appears as both "BOOZ ALLEN HAMILTON INC." and "BOOZ ALLEN HAMILTON INC" without periods. Dollar figures shown are per-entity as recorded; combined corporate figures would be larger.
